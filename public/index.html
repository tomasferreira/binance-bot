How this is calculated
  • Regime uses its own timeframe and candle count (REGIME_TIMEFRAME, REGIME_CANDLES in .env). Default: 1h, 200 bars (~8 days).
  • Volatility — ATR(14) vs its average over the last 50 bars. Ratio > 1.2 = high, < 0.8 = low.
  • Trend — ADX(14): strength of trend. ADX ≥ 25 = trending, < 20 = ranging.
  • Direction — +DI vs −DI. +DI > −DI = bullish, −DI > +DI = bearish.

Glossary

Definitions of financial and statistical terms used in this dashboard and by the strategies.

Indicators

ATR (Average True Range)
Measures volatility by averaging the “true range” of each bar (high minus low, or distance from previous close). Higher ATR = more volatile. Used for volatility regime and in some strategies.
ADX (Average Directional Index)
Measures trend strength (not direction). Values typically 0–100. ADX > 25 often means a strong trend; < 20 means weak or ranging. Used for trend regime.
+DI / −DI (Directional Indicators)
Part of the ADX calculation. +DI captures upward movement, −DI downward. When +DI > −DI the trend is considered bullish; when −DI > +DI, bearish. Used for trend direction.
EMA (Exponential Moving Average)
A moving average that gives more weight to recent prices. EMA(9) reacts quickly; EMA(200) is slow and often used as a long-term trend filter.
MACD (Moving Average Convergence Divergence)
Difference between a fast and a slow EMA (e.g. 12 and 26). A “signal” line (often 9-period EMA of the MACD) is used for crossovers. MACD above zero or crossing above the signal can indicate momentum shifts.
RSI (Relative Strength Index)
Oscillator from 0 to 100 based on recent gains vs losses. RSI > 70 often considered overbought; < 30 oversold. Used in some strategies for entry/exit.
Bollinger Bands
A middle line (e.g. 20-period SMA) with upper and lower bands at a number of standard deviations. Price near the upper band can mean overbought; near the lower band, oversold. Band width reflects volatility.
Stochastic
Compares closing price to the recent high–low range. %K and %D are oscillators (often 0–100). Values above 80 = overbought; below 20 = oversold.

Risk & PnL

PnL (Profit and Loss)
Profit or loss in quote currency (e.g. USDT). Realized = from closed trades. Unrealized = from open positions at current price (mark-to-market).
Stop loss
A price level at which a position is closed to limit loss (e.g. 2% below entry for a long).
Take profit
A price level at which a position is closed to lock in profit (e.g. 4% above entry for a long).
Risk per trade
Fraction of budget (or balance) you are willing to lose on one trade (e.g. 1% = 0.01). Used with stop distance to compute position size.
Exposure
Share of time the strategy has had an open position (e.g. 30% = in a trade 30% of the time since first trade).

Analysis metrics

Win rate
Percentage of closed trades that were profitable (wins ÷ total trades).
Sharpe ratio
Here: average PnL per trade ÷ standard deviation of trade PnLs. Higher = better return per unit of volatility. A simplified version; the classic Sharpe uses returns and risk-free rate.
Sortino ratio
Like Sharpe but only uses downside volatility (std of negative PnLs). Penalizes bad outcomes more than general fluctuation.
Max drawdown
Largest peak-to-trough drop in equity (cumulative PnL). Measures the worst losing run from a previous high.
Profit factor
Gross profit from winning trades ÷ gross loss from losing trades. > 1 = profitable; e.g. 2 means $2 gained per $1 lost.
Expectancy
Average PnL per trade (in quote currency). The amount you expect to make or lose per trade on average.
